Employment agreements are the contractual instruments that bind personnel to security obligations and create legal recourse if those obligations are breached. Policies translate organizational expectations into rules the workforce must follow. Together they convert security from a technical concern into a binding personal obligation. See [[Personnel Security Lifecycle]], [[Organizational Security Processes]].

## Core Agreements

| Agreement | Purpose | Survives Termination? |
|---|---|---|
| **NDA** (Non-Disclosure Agreement) | Protects confidential information disclosed during employment | Yes — typically indefinitely or for a defined post-employment period |
| **NCA** (Non-Compete Agreement) | Restricts joining or starting a competing business after leaving | Yes, but enforceability varies by jurisdiction |
| **AUP** (Acceptable Use Policy) | Defines authorized vs. unauthorized use of company resources | No — applies during employment |
| **Code of Conduct** | Broader ethical and behavioral expectations | No — applies during employment |
| **Conflict of Interest Disclosure** | Ongoing duty to disclose competing interests, outside work, or financial ties | Yes — disclosure obligations continue during employment |

## Key Details

- **NDA** — the most universally enforced agreement. Defines what counts as confidential (often broadly: code, customer data, business strategy, financial info) and the consequences of disclosure. Critical that employees sign *before* receiving any sensitive information.
- **NCA** — heavily jurisdiction-dependent. Some regions (e.g., California) refuse to enforce most non-competes; others enforce them if narrowly scoped in geography, duration, and industry. An overbroad NCA is often unenforceable as a whole.
- **AUP** — operational rules: no personal use of corporate email for business activity, no installing unapproved software, no accessing systems outside job duties. Often referenced when justifying disciplinary action or termination for cause.
- **Code of Conduct** — covers harassment, discrimination, bribery, and conflicts of interest. Less security-specific but supports the broader risk posture.

## Practical Notes

- Agreements should be signed at onboarding, before any access is granted — not retroactively.
- Periodic re-acknowledgement (usually annual) keeps the obligations top-of-mind and creates a fresh evidentiary record.
- Updates to policies require re-acknowledgement; otherwise enforcement against the updated terms is weak.
- For agreements with [[Vendor, Consultant, and Contractor Agreements|third parties]], the same instruments apply but are signed by the contracting entity rather than the individual.
