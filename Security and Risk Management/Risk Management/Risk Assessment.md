Risk assessment identifies and evaluates risk so it can be prioritized and addressed. The two main approaches differ in how they express risk — as relative ratings or as dollar figures. See [[Risk Management Concepts]] for the foundational terminology.

## Quantitative Assessment

Produces dollar-denominated outputs, enabling direct cost-benefit comparisons.

| Term                                | Definition                                            | Formula   |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| **AV** (Asset Value)                | Monetary value of the asset                           | —         |
| **EF** (Exposure Factor)            | Percentage of asset value lost in a single loss event | 0–100%    |
| **SLE** (Single Loss Expectancy)    | Cost of one occurrence of the threat                  | AV × EF   |
| **ARO** (Annual Rate of Occurrence) | Expected frequency of the threat per year             | —         |
| **ALE** (Annual Loss Expectancy)    | Expected annual cost of the risk                      | SLE × ARO |

**Safeguard cost-benefit**: the value of a safeguard is calculated as:

> ALE(pre-control) − ALE(post-control) − ACS = value of safeguard

**ACS (Annual Cost of the Safeguard)** is not just the purchase price — it includes implementation, maintenance, training, and productivity loss from the control being in place. A positive result means the safeguard is worth implementing; a negative result means the cost outweighs the benefit. ACS should be evaluated against what is already in place and what is available on the market.

Quantitative analysis requires reliable data — historical incident rates, accurate asset valuations. Without good inputs, the math produces false precision.

## Qualitative Assessment

Produces relative ratings rather than dollar figures. Suited to situations where data for quantitative analysis is unavailable or unreliable.

**Scenarios** are the foundation of all qualitative techniques. A scenario describes a specific threat event and its potential impact in narrative form. Rather than relying on historical frequency data, assessors reason about each scenario using judgment and expertise. Every technique below is applied per scenario.

- Risks are placed on a **likelihood × impact matrix** using ordinal scales (High/Medium/Low or 1–5)
- Outputs are rankings, not values — useful for prioritization but harder to use for budget justification
- **Delphi technique** — structured anonymous expert consensus used to surface independent judgments and prevent groupthink from skewing results

## Risk Reporting and Documentation

Risk analysis conclusions must be communicated to stakeholders. Reports come in two forms:

- **Internal reports** — for management and risk owners; drive treatment decisions and resource allocation
- **External reports** — for regulators, auditors, or partners; typically more formal and structured

Key artifacts:
- **Risk register** — a living document listing identified risks, their ratings, owners, and treatment decisions
- **Heat map** — a visual likelihood × impact grid that communicates risk posture at a glance
- **Logs** — records of assessments, decisions, and control changes that support audit and accountability

## Semi-Quantitative Assessment

Assigns numeric scores to qualitative scales (e.g., likelihood 1–5 × impact 1–5) to enable ranking without full monetary valuation. Common in practice as a middle ground when full quantitative data is unavailable but relative rankings alone feel insufficient.
